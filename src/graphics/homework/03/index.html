In Texas, Location Means Everything

Narrowing FiveThirtyEight's The Atlas of Redistricting rendering of Texas' congressional districts into a narrative format focusing on racial and political boundaries for my class at Columbia Journalism School.

By Sawyer Click

Published September 15, 2019

Growing up in the boondocks of northeast Texas, it was always obvious everyone around me was voting Republican. As it turns out, browsing through FiveThirtyEight's The Atlas of Redistricting, the entire state bleeds red unless you're in a metropolis or living along the border. Of the 36 districts, only one is actually ever a toss-up.

The substantial political divide stems in part from Texas' concentrated pockets of ethnicities. Most of the districts clinging to the southwest border have a population of majority Hispanic or Latino people. In the northeast, panhandle and parts of northen Central Texas, the districts are overwhelming white.

Metropolises like Dallas and Houston are the only exceptions, largely because they're also not majority white. Both exist outside of the concentrated pocket of Democrat districts alongside the border and are very much Democrat, with FiveThirtyEight predicting over a 99% chance of having a Democrat as a representative.

The largest district in Texas, District 23, straddles the border and is the only competitive district in the state. Constituents of the district have a 76% chance of being represented by someone of color, as the district is majority Hispanic and Latino. However, there is also a 60% chance they will be a Republican. This is in contrast to every other district that has a majority Hispanic and Latino population.

The district is so large that it toys with El Paso on its left-most side and San Antonio on its right-most side, but doesn't dip into either. In all but one iteration of FiveThirtyEight's seven reimagined district shapes the district leans heavily Republican. It only sways to the left if the districts were reimagined to favor Democrats.

This is generally true for most of the district's in Texas. The current congressional boundaries are very near ideal for a republican state. FiveThirtyEight could only predict one more scenario in which Republican's would win over two more boundaries, including District 23.
